Die Hard: It Was A Christmas Movie All Along

by Greta C. Wink

 Early one December morning, my mother, a 66-year-old white woman, shocked the other 66-year-old white people in her feed by posting Run-D.M.C. lyrics as her Facebook status. I admit, I was baffled as well – until my siblings continued the lyrics in her comments. You see, Christmas was coming. Which meant that my mom was getting ready to watch one of her favorite Christmas movies: Die Hard.

        I said in a previous blog that my mom raised me on the old black and white classics that she watched on TV when she was a kid. But she also allowed me to watch a surprising number of incredibly violent action movies like Terminator 2 and Gladiator. For someone who didn’t say a word stronger than “phooey” until I was 14 (and even then, she said “crap”), it feels like a contradiction. My mother is also the person who watched You’ve Got Mail every weekend for a year in 1999. The person who wanted to watch Pocketful of Miracles on my sister’s brand new surround-sound system in 2000, even though my sister was clutching a brand new DVD of The Matrix. The person who wanted to watch the George C. Scott made-for-TV version of A Christmas Carol every year on Christmas Eve even though there were much less boring versions available. Why was it Run-D.M.C. and not Charles Dickens that she was quoting this beautiful winter morn?

The fact is, Die Hard is, and was always intended to be, a Christmas movie. But it isn’t a warm and fuzzy one; it’s a Christmas movie about greed (Gruber), betrayal (Ellis), and corporate sin (Takagi). McClane looks around the luxurious party on the 30th floor of a brand-new building in downtown L.A. and says, “I didn’t realize they celebrated Christmas in Japan.” Of course, Japan does celebrate Christmas - by eating KFC. I imagine that if Jesus had walked into his birthday party at Nakatomi Tower, he would’ve upset some of the tables, too.

Let’s make this one easy to digest with a list. Die Hard is and always has been a Christmas movie because…

  1. …it opens with a Christmas song.
    That Run DMC song is called “Christmas in Hollis,” and it’s played by Argyle (De’voreaux White) as he sits in his limo. We get the song midway through the credits, which run over our active introduction to John and Holly. Argyle turns on the radio. John McClane complains, “don’t you got any Christmas music?” And Argyle answers, “this IS Christmas music.”
  2. …there’s recognition that traveling during the holidays is unpleasant.
    With     Planes, Trains, and Automobiles, this became a mainstay of holiday movies. Do you think fists with your toes would have helped Steve Martin? Probably not.
  3. …John McClane is a fish out of water going through a relationship crisis.
    ^^^Two Christmas movie tropes.
  4. …Holly Gennaro is a career-driven woman with a demanding corporate boss.
    ^^^    three Christmas movie tropes.
  5. …it was released in the run-up to the Christmas season.
    It wasn’t a summer blockbuster; it was released in September. Is September too early for a Christmas movie to be released? Not at all.     Christmas in Connecticut came out in August 1945, Holiday Inn in August 1942, and White Christmas in October 1954.
  6. …it’s about saving a marriage.
    The only reason John McClane is in f***ing California is to reunite his family. During his darkest moment, he delivers a moving monologue about what he needs to say to Holly. (Frankly, some of Bruce Willis’s best acting.) Take John McClane out of a half-finished tower with bleeding feet, put him in a festive scarf and make him interrupt the big Christmas Eve event that Holly is running, and tell me this line of dialogue doesn’t perfectly fit into a Hallmark rom com: “She’s heard me say I love you a thousand times. She never heard me say I’m sorry.”
  7. …HER NAME IS HOLLY, FOR GOSH’S SAKE.
    You think that’s a coincidence?

        Next time some gatekeeping blowhard complains about your enjoyment of Die Hard and its lessons about raging against the violent machine constantly grinding on top of our lives, then you tell them the true message of Die Hard: Become a fly in the ointment. A monkey in the wrench. A pain in the ass. Christ approves.
