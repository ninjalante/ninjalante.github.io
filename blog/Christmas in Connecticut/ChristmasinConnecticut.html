Christmas in Connecticut: The First (and Greatest) Christmas Rom Com

by Greta C. Wink

 My parents are weird. But that worked out for us kids, because we’re all weird too. That’s relevant to this article because instead of watching made-for-kids Christmas movies like The Santa Clause and Jingle All The Way – which my father pronounced as “too stupid” for us – we watched black and white classics and child-inappropriate fare like Die Hard and National Lampoon’s Christmas Vacation. That brings me to the topic at hand: Christmas in Connecticut.
        Released in 1945, just months after the end of World War II, Christmas in Connecticut is a rare comedy for its leading lady, Barbara Stanwyck, who, the previous year, became the highest-paid actress in the United States as a murderous adulteress in Double Indemnity. By this time, Ms. Stanwyck had been acting in talking pictures for 15 years and already received two Oscar nods. At age 38, she was reaching the end of her “leading lady” years - making her the perfect choice for the independent and career-driven columnist Elizabeth Lane, our delightful protagonist.

If “independent and career-driven protagonist” sounds like a familiar Christmas movie trope, you’re not wrong. This is where it started. Elizabeth Lane writes a highly popular cooking, child-rearing, and housekeeping feature in Good Housekeeping, all from the comfort of her beautiful farm in Connecticut. But it’s all a lie: not only does Elizabeth not have a farm, she doesn’t have a husband or a baby either – and she can’t cook! She concocted the persona with her editor to get her the job, and is supported in the cooking department by her “uncle” Felix (played by the delightful S.Z. Sakall, aka the big Hungarian bartender in Casablanca), who writes the recipes and instructions for her so she can incorporate them into her articles. Although her life is a lie, it’s a good living - until her boss (played by the *also* delightful Sydney Greenstreet, aka the big guy in the fez in Casablanca) invites himself and a war hero to her imaginary farm in Connecticut for her to cook them Christmas dinner. She would be fired immediately, except that her love-stricken friend John (Reginald Gardiner, who’s delightfully smarmy but I’ve never seen him in anything else) convinces her to marry him – after he, he owns a large farm in Connecticut, and if she marries him, they can host the boss and the hero, and save her editor’s job.

Oh boy, what a pickle! A #CityFishOutOfWater, a case of #MistakenIdentity, an #OverbearingBoss, a quirky #FriendOrFamilyMember, an #UnwantedRelationship, and a #CharmingCountryLocation – if only there were an insanely handsome stranger for her to fall in love with.

It just so happens that #Romance is on the table too, because when war hero Jefferson Jones (played by the insanely dreamy Dennis Morgan) shows up at the door, it’s love at first sight. Not only is he perfectly handsome and charming, but he’s #GoodWithKids and he #SingsAndPlaysPiano - a trope I normally hate, except that the song he sings was written specifically for the movie (“The Wish That I Wish Tonight.”)

With a little help from her friends, Elizabeth builds lies upon lies as she tries to #KeepHerJob, #GetCloserToTheDreamboat, and #AvoidGettingMarriedToTheRichPutz – which is all I’ll say, because you need to watch this gem yourself.

Other tropes you’ll see? They attend a #SmallTownChristmasEvent and end up #DancingTogether. They go on a romantic #SleighRide. And - another trope that I really, really do hate in every other instance - they #FallDownInTheSnowTogether.

A few fun facts about the movie: one of the writers was a woman, who was in her early 40s when she wrote it. At no point does Elizabeth bemoan being single - in fact, she seems to wallow in it, buying herself an expensive mink coat in her first scene. S.Z. Sakall, who escaped Europe as the Nazis rolled in, speaks with a thick accent. There are two Black actors in small roles, but both speaking parts: one is the delivery woman bringing the mink coat, and the other is a waiter at Uncle Felix’s restaurant. I find this role significant as he provides a textbook (and hilariously deadpan) definition of the word “catastrophe”, which Felix mispronounces for the rest of the film (“CAT-a-STROFE!”).

Though the stakes are high for Elizabeth, the movie itself is pure joy. Her overbearing boss is a jolly addition, her uncle Felix is a grumpy softie, and Stanwyck destroys the “I don’t know how to be a traditional wife and mother” role as she tries to diaper a baby, flip a pancake, and talk about farming. If you’ve ever wondered how the Christmas rom com got started, now you know.

Christmas in Connecticut (1945)

My score: 100/100

directed by Peter Godfrey

starring Barbara Stanwyck, Dennis Morgan, Sydney Greenstreet, and S.Z. Sakall
